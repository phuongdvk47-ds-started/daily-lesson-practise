I. Reading Passage
The Evolution of the Bicycle

The story of personal two-wheeled transport began in 1817 when Baron Karl von Drais, a German inventor, created the Laufmaschine ('running machine'). This device, often called the draisienne or 'dandy horse', was designed as a practical response to a severe shortage of horses caused by crop failures after the Mount Tambora volcanic eruption. Lacking pedals, gears, and chains, the vehicle required riders to sit on a wooden frame and push off the ground using their feet. Although it was primarily enjoyed by wealthy young men, an original model preserved as an artifact [R] in museum collections shows that it established the essential principle of balancing on two wheels.

Major improvements were introduced in the 1860s in France, where bicycle makers added rotary cranks and pedals onto the front wheel axle. This new machine, known as the velocipede or 'boneshaker', allowed riders to move forward without touching the ground. Because the rigid iron tires and wooden wheels caused uncomfortable vibrations on cobblestone roads, the ride was notoriously rough. Nevertheless, mass production began, turning the velocipede into a popular exhibit [R] at public fairs across Europe and North America.

By the 1870s, engineers recognized that enlarging the front wheel increased the distance covered per pedal stroke, which boosted overall speed. This gave birth to the iconic 'penny-farthing', featuring a massive front wheel up to 1.5 meters tall and a tiny rear wheel. While riding high above the street provided speed and status, stopping abruptly often threw the cyclist forward over the handlebars. Consequently, cycling during this era remained a daring sport for courageous individuals rather than a daily commuting option for the general public.

The true revolution in everyday transport arrived in the late 1880s with John Kemp Starley's 'Safety Bicycle'. Featuring two equal-sized wheels, a rear-wheel chain drive, and inflatable pneumatic rubber tires invented by John Boyd Dunlop, the new bicycle was comfortable, stable, and easy to ride. It allowed women and working-class citizens unprecedented mobility, transforming urban transport into a diverse collection [R] of sustainable personal vehicles that reshaped modern society.
