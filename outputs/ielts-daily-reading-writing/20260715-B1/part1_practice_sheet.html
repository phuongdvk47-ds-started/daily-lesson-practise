I. Reading Passage
Volunteering and Brain Health in Later Life

Volunteering in late life is associated with better cognitive function — specifically, better executive function and episodic memory. According to a new study from UC Davis Health presented at the Alzheimer’s Association International Conference (AAIC) 2023 in Amsterdam, regular community participation can act as a natural defense. Researchers found that volunteering was associated with higher baseline scores on tests of executive function, which includes tasks like planning, focusing, and solving everyday problems. Additionally, it improves verbal episodic memory, which is the ability to remember lists of words or events. While the widespread adoption [R] of such volunteering programs has yet to be realized globally, early findings suggest they offer key protective benefits.

The participants in the study had an average age of 74. They were part of two larger studies: the Kaiser Healthy Aging and Diverse Life Experiences Study (KHANDLE) and the Study of Healthy Aging in African Americans (STAR). The group was highly diverse, consisting of 48% Black, 20% white, 17% Asian, and 14% Latino participants. Previous research on volunteering and brain health has focused mainly on non-Latino white populations. Including diverse backgrounds is critical because some communities may be more vulnerable [R] to cognitive decline due to socioeconomic factors. Thus, promoting inclusive volunteer opportunities can address disparities.

Researchers also examined if volunteering was associated with cognitive decline over a follow-up period of 1.2 years. While they observed positive trends, the association did not reach statistical significance. However, scientists believe volunteering protects the brain by offering cognitive stimulation, social interaction, and physical activity. By engaging in volunteer work, older adults can seamlessly [R] integrate physical movement and social engagement into their weekly routines. Those who volunteered several times per week demonstrated the highest levels of executive function, highlighting the value of regular community participation. Ultimately, volunteerism could serve as a simple, low-cost intervention to protect against the risk of Alzheimer's disease and related dementias.

Source: Adapted from UC Davis Health - "Volunteering in late life may protect the brain against cognitive decline and dementia" (2023-07-20)
