I. Reading Passage
Exercise for the Lazy: Finding the Minimum for Good Health

Many people start the year with a New Year's resolution to get fit. They often promise themselves that they will join a gym or start a structured exercise program, such as swimming or running. However, after a few weeks, many of these promises are broken. People find that they lack the motivation to keep going, or they are simply too busy.

Is it possible to stay healthy without doing a lot of hard work? In a recent BBC programme, Neil and Sam discussed a "lazy person's guide" to fitness. Health experts used to recommend at least thirty minutes of moderate physical activity five times a week. For many people, this feels like a big challenge. But new research suggests that even a small amount of activity can benefit your health.

For instance, studies show that short, intense bursts of movement during the day can protect your cardiovascular system. Instead of spending hours on a treadmill, you can incorporate activity into your daily routine. This strategy is sometimes called "habit stacking." For example, you can do some stretches while waiting for your coffee to brew, or take the stairs instead of the elevator.

According to government statistics, people who have a sedentary lifestyle—meaning they spend most of the day sitting at a desk or watching television—have a higher risk of heart disease. To prevent this, experts recommend setting a target of ten thousand steps a day. However, even if you do not reach this target, walking just a short distance is much better than doing nothing.

The duration of your exercise is less important than being consistent. If you exercise regularly, even for just ten minutes at a time, you will see a positive impact on your health. Finding an activity that you enjoy is the key. When exercise is fun, it does not feel like a chore, and you are more likely to make it a permanent part of your life.
