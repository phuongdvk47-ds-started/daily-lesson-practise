I. Reading Passage
City Life Versus Countryside Life

Towns and cities are called 'urban' areas, and the areas around them are called 'suburban'. For example, Holywood is a suburban town of Belfast, and people who live there 'live in the suburbs'. Further away from the urban and suburban areas, you reach 'rural' areas — the countryside. It is in the countryside that you find villages and hamlets, which are very small settlements.

There are not so many jobs in rural areas, and this is why they have smaller settlements. In the countryside there are fewer shops, no cinemas, and maybe only one bus a day. Public transport is limited, so people often need a car to travel to larger towns for work or shopping.

However, the countryside also has clear advantages for daily life. There is more open space and cleaner air than in the city. Many people say that living close to nature improves their wellbeing, because the quiet environment helps them feel calm and relaxed after a busy week.

Cities, by contrast, offer more opportunities for work and study. There are far more jobs, a wider choice of shops, and plenty of cinemas, restaurants, and nightlife. Yet city life is not perfect. The noise, traffic, and crowds can increase a person's stress, and some people find the fast pace exhausting.

Another difference is focus and learning, which affect school and jobs. In a quiet village, there are fewer distractions, so it can be easier to concentrate on study or work. Good concentration often improves when the surroundings are peaceful. In a busy city, by comparison, constant noise can make deep focus harder.

In the end, both ways of living have advantages and disadvantages. Whether you live in a big town or a small village, the right choice depends on what matters most to you — convenience and excitement, or space and calm. There is no single answer that suits everyone.
