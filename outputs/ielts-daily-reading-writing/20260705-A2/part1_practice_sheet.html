I. Reading Passage
Planning a Simple Bus Trip

Mina wants to visit a community sports centre by bus. Before she leaves home, she uses an online [R] planner to plan a trip. She types her starting address and the destination, then chooses the date and time. The planner shows the best route and tells her where to get on and off. Saving this search becomes a weekly habit [R].

At the street, Mina looks for a bus stop. It is marked by a pole and a sign. The sign lists the route number, and the same number appears on the front of the bus. She also checks the direction, because the same route can travel two ways. She waits on the pavement; some stops have a shelter, but some do not.

On the bus, Mina taps her ticket card and sits near the middle. She watches the screen and listens for announcements. When her next bus stop is announced, she can press the red stop button. A light says STOP REQUESTED, so the driver knows. Mina should leave through the side door and look both ways.

A schedule is useful, but real streets can change. If the bus is late, Mina checks online [R] arrival times or reads the printed schedule. If she rides in the wrong direction, she gets off at a safe stop, crosses at the traffic light, and checks the map again. A calm two-minute check can make a big difference [R] and help a new rider feel independent.
