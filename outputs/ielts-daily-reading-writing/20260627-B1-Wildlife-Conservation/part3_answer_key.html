III. Writing Guidance / Suggested Answers
Task 1: Rearrange the words
  • a) Zimbabwe is moving wild animals to the north.
  • Teacher's Guidance: Hello! Let's look at how we build this sentence together. In English, we always start with the Subject (who or what does the action?). Here, our subject is "Zimbabwe". Next, we need the Verb (what does it do?). The action is "is moving". Next, we add the Object (who or what is being moved?) which is "wild animals". Finally, we specify the place or direction: "to the north". Put them together, capitalize the first letter, add a period at the end, and you get: Zimbabwe is moving wild animals to the north.
  • Self-Correction Tip: Check if you capitalized "Zimbabwe" and put a period . at the end of the sentence.
  • b) Drought is a big threat to elephants.
  • Teacher's Guidance: Let's think about this step-by-step. Our subject is "Drought". What is it? We use the linking verb "is". Next, we add the complement "a big threat" (adjective "big" comes before the noun "threat"). Lastly, we state who is affected: "to elephants". Combining these gives us: Drought is a big threat to elephants.
  • Self-Correction Tip: Check that "threat" is spelled correctly and that you used the preposition "to" for the target.
  • c) Rangers protect wildlife in national parks.
  • Teacher's Guidance: Great job! Let's identify the parts of speech. "Rangers" is the plural subject. The action verb is "protect". What do they protect? "wildlife" (object). Where? "in national parks". This gives us: Rangers protect wildlife in national parks.
  • Self-Correction Tip: Verify that "rangers" has an 's' at the end for plural, and "protect" has no 's' (since the subject is plural).
Task 2: Combine these sentences
  • a) The park has little water, so the animals destroy their habitat. (or: The animals destroy their habitat because the park has little water.)
  • Teacher's Guidance: Let's look at these two ideas: (1) The park has little water (the cause) and (2) The animals destroy their habitat (the result). Since there is a cause-and-effect relationship, we can combine them in two ways:
  • Using "so": we state the cause first, add a comma , and "so", and then the result. (The park has little water, so the animals destroy their habitat.)
  • Using "because": we state the result first, add "because" (no comma!), and then the cause. (The animals destroy their habitat because the park has little water.)
  • Self-Correction Tip: Double-check if you used a comma before "so", or no comma before "because".
  • b) Rangers want to save the giraffes, but the journey is very long.
  • Teacher's Guidance: Let's look at these two sentences: (1) Rangers want to save the giraffes (a positive hope) and (2) The journey is very long (a difficulty or obstacle). Because these two ideas show a contrast or obstacle, the best connector to link them is "but". Remember to place a comma , before "but". This gives us: Rangers want to save the giraffes, but the journey is very long.
  • Self-Correction Tip: Ensure you placed the comma , before "but".
  • c) Lions live in Sapi Reserve and zebras live there too.
  • Teacher's Guidance: Here, both sentences are adding similar information about two types of animals living in the same reserve. To add related ideas, we use "and". We can write: Lions live in Sapi Reserve and zebras live there too. Notice that we use the word "too" at the end of the sentence to reinforce the addition.
  • Self-Correction Tip: Make sure you capitalized the name "Sapi Reserve" properly.
Task 3: Descriptive writing (Favorite wild animal)

Suggested Model Answer:

"My favorite wild animal is the elephant. Elephants are very large and have a long nose called a trunk. They usually live in hot forests and grasslands in Africa and Asia. I like them because they are very smart and friendly." (40 words)
  • Teacher's Guidance: Let's learn how to write a descriptive paragraph of an animal! To make your thoughts clear and structured, you can follow this 4-step outline:

1. Introduce the animal: State clearly what your favorite wild animal is. (My favorite wild animal is the...)

2. Describe its appearance: Mention its size, color, or special features (like a trunk or long neck). (Elephants are very large and have...)

3. Describe its habitat: Say where they live in the wild. (They usually live in...)

4. Give your reason: Explain why you like them. (I like them because...)

By writing one simple sentence for each step, you will naturally create a cohesive paragraph of about 40 words!

  • Common Errors to Avoid:
  • Subject-Verb Agreement: Write "Elephants are..." (plural subject) and "They live..." (plural pronoun), not "Elephants is..." or "They lives...".
  • Spelling: Pay attention to the spelling of "favorite" and "grasslands".
Task 4: Describing places (Public park)

Suggested Model Answer:

"There is a beautiful public park near my house. It has many green trees, colorful flowers, and a small lake. People can walk, run, and play sports there. In the evening, children like to ride bicycles and play games." (40 words)
  • Teacher's Guidance: Let's practice describing a public park! When writing a description of a place, a great cognitive technique is to start "wide" and then zoom in on specific details. Let's try this structure:

1. Introduce the place: Say what and where it is. (There is a beautiful public park near my house.)

2. Describe its features: Mention what it has (trees, flowers, lakes). (It has many green trees...)

3. Describe people's actions: Explain what activities people do there. (People can walk, run...)

4. Mention a specific time: Add detail about what happens at a specific time, like evening or morning. (In the evening, children like to...)

This simple four-sentence template helps you build a beautiful, logical paragraph effortlessly!

  • Common Errors to Avoid:
  • There is vs There are: Use "There is" for a singular noun (There is a park) and "There are" for plural nouns (There are many trees).
  • Lists: When listing items, remember to use commas and "and" before the last item (trees, flowers, and a lake).
Task 5: (*) Simple Email / Invitation (Wildlife park)

Suggested Model Answer:

"Hi John,
Would you like to go to the wildlife park with me next Saturday? We can see many elephants, giraffes, and zebras. We can travel there by bus. Let's meet at the bus station at 8:00 AM.
Write back soon!
Best,
Nam" (48 words)
  • Teacher's Guidance: Writing an invitation email is a vital real-life skill and forms the foundation for IELTS General Writing Task 1. Let's learn the standard structure of an informal email:

1. The Greeting: Keep it friendly and simple. (Hi John,)

2. The Invite (Main Goal): Ask the invitation question directly using a polite phrase. (Would you like to go to... with me next Saturday?)

3. The Details: Explain what you will do/see, and how you will travel. (We can see... We can travel there by...)

4. The Meeting Time & Place: Suggest a specific time and location to meet. (Let's meet at... at 8:00 AM.)

5. The Call to Action: Ask them to reply to you. (Write back soon!)

6. The Sign-off & Name: Close warmly. (Best, followed by your name on the next line).

Whenever you write an email, check off these 6 elements one by one. This will guarantee your writing is fully structured and professional!

  • Common Errors to Avoid:
  • Punctuation: Remember to place a comma after the greeting (Hi John,) and the closing (Best,).
  • Capitalization: Capitalize days of the week (Saturday), names (John, Nam), and the first word of every new line.
  • Time Prepositions: Use the preposition "at" before times (at 8:00 AM) and "on" before days (on Saturday).
