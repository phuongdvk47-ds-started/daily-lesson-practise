I. Reading Passage
Climate Agency: 2023 Broke Global Heat Record

Earth shattered global heat records in 2023, flirting with the 1.5-degree Celsius warming limit set by international agreements. The European climate agency, Copernicus, reported that the average global temperature last year was the warmest in recorded history.

Copernicus reported that the average temperature for 2023 was 14.98 degrees Celsius. That number is about one-sixth of a degree Celsius higher than the previous record set in 2016.

Samantha Burgess, the deputy director of Copernicus, said the record-breaking warmth lasted for seven consecutive months. "It wasn’t just a season or a month that was exceptional. It was exceptional for over half the year," she said.

Scientists have noted that 2023 was likely the warmest period for the Earth in over 100,000 years. For more than half of the year, the world was 1.5 degrees Celsius warmer than pre-industrial levels, which is the average temperature from the mid-1800s.

Malte Meinshausen, a climate scientist at the University of Melbourne, explained that about 1.3 degrees Celsius of the warming is driven by greenhouse gases. Another 0.1 degrees Celsius comes from El Niño, a natural weather pattern that warms parts of the Pacific Ocean. The rest is from other smaller factors.

The 1.5-degree Celsius limit was established in the 2015 Paris climate conference. Climate experts say exceeding this limit on a long-term basis could lead to extreme climate disasters. Although the global average for 2023 was just below 1.5 degrees Celsius, Burgess urged countries to take action. "We have to keep the goal alive because lives are at risk," she said.
