I. Reading Passage
Settlements in the UK: Urban, Suburban, and Rural Life

Settlements in the United Kingdom are divided into three main types. Towns and cities are called 'urban' areas. The residential zones around them are known as 'suburban' areas, or suburbs. For example, Holywood is a suburban town of Belfast, and people who live there say they live in the suburbs. In urban areas, people have easy access to shops, hospitals, and schools. However, living in cities requires high cognitive [R] effort because of constant noise and traffic.

Further away from the urban zones, you find 'rural' areas, also known as the countryside. Here, you can find villages and hamlets, which are very small settlements. There are not many jobs in rural areas, which is why they are smaller. Many children in rural schools do not have options for special activities, such as musical training [R], because teachers and facilities are limited.

There are advantages and disadvantages to both environments. The countryside has fewer shops, no cinemas, and maybe only one bus service a day. However, it offers more open space and cleaner air. Studies show that spending time in a quiet countryside neighborhood can improve children's working memory [R] and concentration. In contrast, cities offer more jobs and entertainment, but they are often crowded and noisy.
