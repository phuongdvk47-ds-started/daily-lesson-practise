I. Reading Passage
Leaving the Mekong Delta for the Big City

Eleven-year-old twins Dao Bao Tran and Do Hoang Trung live with their grandmother on a small houseboat in Vietnam's Mekong Delta. Although they have big dreams—Tran loves K-pop and wants to visit Seoul, while Trung hopes to become a singer—their reality is difficult. Trung says their dreams are unrealistic, adding, "I know I will end up going to the city to try and make a living."

In recent years, climate change and human activities have begun to disrupt [R] traditional livelihoods in the Mekong Delta. A United Nations report warned that the region faces severe floods in the wet season and extreme droughts in the dry season. Upstream dams have reduced the water flow, while rising sea levels have caused salt water to ruin farm soil. Consequently, the delta's share of Vietnam's GDP has dropped from 27 percent in 1990 to less than 18 percent in 2019. Because of this economic decline, many families feel frustrated [R] by their lack of options.

To escape poverty, many residents choose to migrate. Soon after the twins were born, their mother, Do Thi Son Ca, left for Ho Chi Minh City to work in a clothing factory. However, moving to the city does not guarantee success. Ca recently lost her job due to a drop in international clothing orders and had to move back to the countryside. Meanwhile, the twins have already missed two years of school because their grandmother could not afford the fees.

Despite these hardships, the draw of Ho Chi Minh City remains strong, especially for the young. Some students must prioritize [R] helping their families over their own education. For example, 23-year-old Trung Hieu moved to the city to work in a factory and drive a motorcycle taxi. He did this so he could send money home to pay for his younger sister's school. While Vietnam's government has approved plans to modernize the agricultural economy in the delta, moving to the city remains the fastest way out of poverty for many.
