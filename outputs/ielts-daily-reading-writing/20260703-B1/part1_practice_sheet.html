I. Reading Passage
Safer Streets for Everyone

The World Health Organization reports that about 1.19 million people die each year because of road traffic crashes. These deaths are not spread equally around the world. About 92% of road fatalities happen in low- and middle-income countries, even though these countries have only around 60% of the world's vehicles. The problem also affects young people strongly: road injuries are a leading cause of death for children and young adults aged 5 to 29.

A road is used by many groups, but some have less protection than others. More than half of all road traffic deaths are among vulnerable road users, including pedestrians, cyclists and motorcyclists. For this reason, a safe street is not only a place for cars. It is a shared community [R] space where people should be able to walk, cycle, ride or wait for a bus without facing unnecessary danger.

The WHO recommends the safe system approach, which accepts that people can make mistakes. Instead of expecting perfect behaviour, the system should reduce the chance that human error becomes a tragedy. This approach depends on safe speeds, safer vehicles, safer roads and safer road users. It also asks drivers, planners and local leaders to stay accountable [R] for decisions that affect other people's lives.

Risk rises when people travel too fast or pay attention to the wrong thing. The WHO explains that higher average speed increases both the chance of a crash and the seriousness of the result. Distracted driving is another danger. Drivers using mobile phones are about four times more likely to be involved in a crash; phone use slows reaction times, affects responses to traffic signals, and makes it harder to keep proper following distances.

Safer streets also need better road infrastructure. Footpaths, cycling lanes, safe crossing points and traffic calming measures can reduce risk for people outside vehicles. Good transport planning places bus stops, crossings and school gates where they are easy to see and reach. A simple crossing can become an important safety resource [R] when it helps students, older people and cyclists move through traffic more confidently.

Finally, rules matter only when they are used. The WHO says traffic laws and penalties must be regularly updated and enforced. Law enforcement, public awareness and education all help reduce fatal crashes and serious injuries. The main lesson is clear: safer roads are created by many small actions working together, not by one sign or one rule alone.
