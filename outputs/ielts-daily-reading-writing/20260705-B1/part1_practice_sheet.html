I. Reading Passage
College Success: Joining Campus Groups to Feel Connected

The transition to college life is an exciting journey, but it can also be a challenging and lonely time for many new students. Leaving familiar support systems behind often leads to feelings of homesickness. However, Dr. Matt Couch, an Associate Dean of Students at The Ohio State University, notes that campuses offer a vibrant community outside of the lecture hall. Joining a student organization is one of the best ways for students to meet people, foster friendships, and build a strong sense of belonging early in their academic journey.

Many organizations focus on academic fields or professional development, which are highly beneficial for students looking to prepare for their future careers. For example, joining a business society or an economics club allows members to hear from experts, attend networking events, and apply their classroom knowledge to real-world projects. Through these activities, students develop crucial soft skills—such as leadership, communication, and teamwork—which can make them much more appealing to a prospective employer [R].

For other students, casual social clubs based on creative hobbies are a necessary escape from academic pressures. Whether participating in a creative writing workshop or a recreational sports club, students find a positive outlet to manage stress and navigate [R] the demands of university life. These extracurricular activities offer a healthy balance, helping to prevent burnout and giving students a much-needed mental break between exams.

Universities recognize the value of these organizations and often support them by providing financial assistance or meeting spaces. To ensure these student groups stay aligned with university policies, they are typically required to have a faculty advisor who guides their activities. This structured guidance helps student leaders learn project management and maintain a high level of commitment [R] to their organization's long-term goals.

Ultimately, getting involved in campus life is an investment in both personal and academic success. Research indicates that active students show a higher likelihood of completing their degrees and maintaining a strong academic performance. Dr. Couch suggests that if students cannot find a club that matches their specific interests, they should establish their own. By taking this initiative, they can build a customized support system that enriches their entire university experience.
