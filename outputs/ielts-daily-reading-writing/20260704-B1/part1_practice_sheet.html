I. Reading Passage
Understanding Your Rights: Five Off-Limit Interview Questions

When entering the job market, every job seeker must learn how to navigate the interview process. During these meetings, a prospective employer wants to evaluate your professional credentials to see if you fit their corporate culture. However, many candidates do not know that there are strict laws governing what an interviewer can ask. In the United States, federal anti-discrimination laws protect applicants from unfair questioning. This means that both companies and hiring managers should stay accountable [R] for the questions they ask, ensuring they relate only to your ability to do the work.

One of the most common areas of confusion involves marital status and family plans. Under federal guidelines, questions about whether you are married, have children, or plan to start a family are strictly off-limits. Prospective employers cannot ask about your childcare arrangements, as this information has historically been used to discriminate against women. Although an employer might want to know if you can commit to a long-term project, they must ask about your commitment and availability directly (e.g., "Can you work from 9 a.m. to 5 p.m.?") rather than asking about your personal life.

Another major protected area is religion. It is illegal for an employer to ask about your religious beliefs or which holidays you observe. While they can ask if you are available to work on weekends, they cannot ask about the specific reason you might need a Sunday off. If a company requires employees to work during a holiday, they must explain the schedule and ask if you can fulfill that requirement. Asking about your faith is a direct violation that creates serious legal liability for the firm.

Furthermore, questions regarding citizenship and national origin are prohibited. An interviewer cannot ask "Where were you born?" or "Is English your first language?" However, they can legally ask if you are authorized to work in the United States. Unfortunately, a general lack of public awareness [R] means that many applicants feel forced to share their nationality or native language. Knowing your rights ensures that you only discuss your qualifications rather than personal details that are not relevant to the job.

Finally, questions about age or physical health are protected. Employers cannot ask how old you are, nor can they ask about your medical history. They may only ask if you can perform the essential functions of the job, with or without reasonable accommodation. Understanding these protections helps prevent human error [R] during your job search. By keeping the conversation professional, both candidates and employers can make fair decisions based on talent and experience rather than bias.
