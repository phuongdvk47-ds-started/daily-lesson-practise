III. Writing Guidance / Suggested Answers
Task 1: Rearrange the words
  • a) The veterinary hospital saves wild animals.
  • Teacher's Guidance: Hello! Let's look at how we build this sentence together. In English, we always start with the Subject (who or what does the action?). Here, our subject is "The veterinary hospital". Next, we need the Verb (what does it do?). The action is "saves". Finally, we add the Object (who or what is being saved?). That is "wild animals". Put them together, capitalize the first letter, add a period at the end, and you get: The veterinary hospital saves wild animals. Easy, right?
  • Self-Correction Tip: Check if you capitalized "The" and put a period . at the end of the sentence.
  • b) Otters live in their natural surroundings.
  • Teacher's Guidance: Wonderful! Let's think about this step-by-step. Our subject here is "Otters". What is their action? They "live". Where do they live? "in their natural surroundings". When we combine these parts, we get a complete and descriptive sentence: Otters live in their natural surroundings.
  • Self-Correction Tip: Make sure you spelled "surroundings" correctly with an 's' at the end.
  • c) Doctors care for sick animals.
  • Teacher's Guidance: Great job! Let's identify the parts of speech. "Doctors" is the plural subject. The action verb is a phrase: "care for". Who do they care for? "sick animals" (the adjective "sick" must come before the noun "animals"). This gives us: Doctors care for sick animals.
  • Self-Correction Tip: Verify that "care for" is written as two separate words and that "sick" is placed before "animals".
Task 2: Combine these sentences
  • a) The python is twelve feet long, but it has no teeth. (or: The python is twelve feet long but it has no teeth.)
  • Teacher's Guidance: Let's look at these two ideas: (1) The python is twelve feet long (a positive/neutral fact) and (2) It has no teeth (a surprising/contrasting fact). Because these two ideas show a contrast, the best connector to link them is "but". In English, we place a comma , right before "but" to separate the two clauses cleanly. Now we have: The python is twelve feet long, but it has no teeth.
  • Self-Correction Tip: Ensure you don't repeat the subject "python" twice; instead, use the pronoun "it" in the second clause.
  • b) The monkey was crying because it lost its mother.
  • Teacher's Guidance: Let's think about the relationship between these two sentences: (1) The monkey was crying (the result/action) and (2) It lost its mother (the cause or reason). When one sentence explains why the other happened, we connect them using "because". Note that in English, we do not put a comma before "because" when it is in the middle of a sentence. This gives us: The monkey was crying because it lost its mother.
  • Self-Correction Tip: Remember not to write a comma before "because". Also, check the spelling of the possessive pronoun "its" (no apostrophe!).
  • c) Nicci Wright works at the hospital and Karin Lourens works there too.
  • Teacher's Guidance: Here, both sentences are adding similar information about two doctors working in the same place. To add related ideas, we use "and". We can write: Nicci Wright works at the hospital and Karin Lourens works there too. Notice that we use the word "too" at the end of the sentence to reinforce the addition.
  • Self-Correction Tip: Make sure you capitalized the names "Nicci Wright" and "Karin Lourens" properly.
Task 3: Descriptive writing (Favorite animal)

Suggested Model Answer:

"My favorite animal is the cat. My cat is white and grey. It has beautiful green eyes. It loves to eat fish and drink milk every day." (26 words)
  • Teacher's Guidance: Let's learn how to write a simple description of an animal! To make your thoughts clear and structured, you can follow this 3-step outline:

1. Introduce the animal: State clearly what your favorite animal is. (My favorite animal is the...)

2. Describe its appearance: Mention its color, size, or special features like eyes or ears. (It is white and grey. It has...)

3. Describe its habits: Mention what it likes to eat, drink, or do. (It loves to eat...)

By writing one simple sentence for each step, you will naturally create a cohesive paragraph of about 25 words!

  • Common Errors to Avoid:
  • Subject-Verb Agreement: Write "It loves..." or "It eats..." (with an 's' for singular "it"), not "It love..." or "It eat...".
  • Adjective Order: Always put colors before nouns (e.g., "green eyes", not "eyes green").
Task 4: Describing places (Neighborhood)

Suggested Model Answer:

"I live in a small, quiet neighborhood in the countryside. There is a big green park near my house. Many colorful birds and dogs play there." (25 words)
  • Teacher's Guidance: Let's practice describing your neighborhood! When writing a description of a place, a great cognitive technique is to start "wide" and then zoom in on a specific detail. Let's try this structure:

1. The wide picture: Say where you live and describe the general feel of the area. (I live in a small, quiet neighborhood...)

2. The focal point: Zoom in on one specific feature, especially a place where nature or animals are present. (There is a big green park near my house.)

3. The action: Mention what animals or people do there to bring your description to life! (Many colorful birds and dogs play there.)

This simple three-sentence template helps you build a beautiful, logical paragraph effortlessly!

  • Common Errors to Avoid:
  • There is vs There are: Use "There is" for a singular noun (There is a park) and "There are" for plural nouns (There are many trees).
  • Spelling: Pay attention to the spelling of "neighborhood" and "countryside".
Task 5: (*) Informal email / invitation (Animal shelter)

Suggested Model Answer:

"Hi Lan,
Would you like to visit a local animal shelter with me next Sunday? We can see many cute puppies and kittens. Let's meet at the bus station at 9:00 AM.
Write back soon!
Best,
Nam" (37 words)
  • Teacher's Guidance: Writing an invitation email is a vital real-life skill and forms the foundation for IELTS General Writing Task 1. Let's learn the standard structure of an informal email:

1. The Greeting: Keep it friendly and simple. (Hi Lan,)

2. The Invite (Main Goal): Ask the invitation question directly using a polite phrase. (Would you like to visit... with me?)

3. The Details: Explain what you will do or see, and suggest a specific time and location to meet. (We can see... Let's meet at...)

4. The Call to Action: Ask them to reply to you. (Write back soon!)

5. The Sign-off & Name: Close warmly. (Best, followed by your name on the next line).

Whenever you write an email, check off these 5 elements one by one. This will guarantee your writing is fully structured and professional!

  • Common Errors to Avoid:
  • Punctuation: Remember to place a comma after the greeting (Hi Lan,) and the closing (Best,).
  • Capitalization: Capitalize days of the week (Sunday), names (Lan, Nam), and the first word of every new line.
  • Time Prepositions: Use the preposition "at" before times (at 9:00 AM) and "on" before days (on Sunday).
