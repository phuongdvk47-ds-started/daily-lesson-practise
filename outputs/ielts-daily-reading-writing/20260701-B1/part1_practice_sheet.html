I. Reading Passage
Public Schools Take Legal Action Against Social Media Companies

Social media companies are facing legal action seeking to hold them accountable for the youth mental health crisis. Recently, public school districts in Seattle, Washington, and a suburban school system filed lawsuits against the tech companies behind TikTok, Instagram, Facebook, YouTube, and Snapchat. They argue that these platforms have created a public nuisance by intentionally targeting their products to children.

The school districts blame these companies for a rise in mental health and behavioral disorders, including anxiety, depression, disordered eating, and cyberbullying. They argue that the rise of a sedentary [R] lifestyle, where teenagers spend hours glued to screens, has had a negative impact on their well-being. These growing mental health problems have made it much harder to educate students, forcing schools to hire more mental health professionals and create special lessons about the effects of social media.

However, the tech industry has pushed back. NetChoice, a tech trade association, argues that companies should not be held responsible because they cannot control user behavior. They compare the lawsuits to suing a bookstore because a clerk recommended a book that made a teenager feel sad. Furthermore, industry experts point out that social media can actually benefit [R] teenagers by helping them connect with peers and access educational tools.

These lawsuits face a complex legal path due to federal laws that typically protect internet companies from liability. In the United States, Section 230 protects online platforms from being sued over what users post. However, the schools argue that this protection should not apply in this case, because the companies' own algorithms actively promote and push harmful content to minors. While the legal battle is expected to be long and difficult, school advocates hope that new regulations will finally prevent [R] tech companies from exposing children to online dangers.
