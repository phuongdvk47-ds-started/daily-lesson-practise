I. Reading Passage
Employers Hesitant to Hire Gen Z College Graduates

According to a recent report by a higher education advising organization, nearly forty percent of American employers avoid offering jobs to recent college graduates. These business leaders say that members of Generation Z, or "Gen Z" (born between 1997 and 2012), make serious mistakes during interviews. Instead, many executives prefer to hire older workers who they believe work harder and have better professional manners [R].

In a survey of 800 managers and executives, almost sixty percent reported that recent college graduates are unprepared for the workforce, and half admitted they had to fire a young worker. Some managers complained about interview behaviors, such as candidates refusing to turn on their cameras during video interviews, or selecting noisy public areas for calls. Many employers worry that productivity will worsen [R] if they hire people who cannot meet deadlines.

Experts suggest that the COVID-19 pandemic disrupted key activities that prepare students for careers. During the pandemic, strict quarantine restrictions [R] were put in place, forcing schools online. As a result, many students missed out on internships and the chance to meet professionals. Furthermore, some became used to missing deadlines because teachers eased rules during online classes. In the workplace, however, managers are not as permissive.

Career directors argue that while Gen Z students are highly skilled in online communication and data management, they need help developing "soft skills." Soft skills include business etiquette, such as making formal introductions, offering a firm handshake, and knowing how to follow up after a meeting. While some managers remain frustrated with younger workers, others suggest that companies should be willing to coach new employees. Despite these criticisms, some career experts believe that Gen Z might create a positive shift, encouraging companies to offer higher salaries and better flexibility for everyone.

Source: Adapted from VOA Learning English - "US Businesses Concerned about Hiring Young, 'Gen Z' Workers" (January 19, 2024)
